The reality of the ballpoint pen 

Introduction 
Regarding the "where challenge" we have chosen an everyday object that might be in your hand right now or it is laying motionless on your work table. We use this every single day but still aren't able to recognize the importance of it. Coming straight to the point, we wanted to unravel the true identity of the ballpoint pen and it has revealed numerous amounts of intriguing information. The first ballpoint pen was made by a leather manufacturer in 1888 because the regular writing utensils such as the fountain pen didn't work on leather. 

The different Parts of the pen 
A pen is made up of components that come from all around the world. The basic components that create the pen as a whole is made up of a spring, ink cartridge, and the small ball at the front.

The Spring
A small strand of aluminum is tightly coiled leaving an approximate of 5 mm of spacing in between each loop. This small feature gives the pen its ability to retract and reveal the ink cartridge. 

Ink Cartridge
In it's simplest form, the ink we write with is a pigment or dye and a binder. To hold this ink (pigment), usually in regular pens a cartridge is placed around the ink and prevents the substance inside from leaking. Isaac Newtons law of gravity  F = G*((m sub 1*m sub 2)/r^2), come  in play for something as simple as a pen. Having a force of 9.807 m/s², the ink flows steadily down the cartridge and slowly flows down to the ball at the bottom of the pen. If a regular pen was taken into space, the force of gravity is so minuscule it virtually feels as if there is not gravity. However, using this knowledge the pen won't work due to the fact the ink will not be forced out. 

The Ball
A small tungsten carbide ball, about 0.7 mm in diameter is what makes the pen have it's fluid strokes of ink. In its most basic form, tungsten carbide is a fine gray powder, but it is malleable so it can be formed into shapes for use in industrial machinery, cutting tools, abrasives, armor-piercing rounds, and jewelry. An interesting fact is tungsten carbide is approximately two times stiffer than steel and it is comparable with corundum in hardness. 
Now that you know the basic components of the ballpoint pen, we wanted to go more in depth and research about the small ​tungsten carbide ball that gave the pen its name.
